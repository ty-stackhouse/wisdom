
# EU ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE ACT (EU AI ACT) - COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The **European Union Artificial Intelligence Act (EU AI Act)** is the world's first comprehensive legal framework regulating artificial intelligence systems. Published in the Official Journal of the European Union on July 12, 2024, and entering into force on August 1, 2024, the Act establishes a risk-based regulatory approach to AI governance. The regulation applies to organizations globally that develop, deploy, or use AI systems within the European Union or that affect EU citizens, regardless of where the provider is located.

The Act categorizes AI systems into distinct risk levels—unacceptable, high, limited, and minimal risk—with corresponding obligations ranging from outright prohibition to minimal transparency requirements. It also introduces specific provisions for General Purpose AI (GPAI) models, with enhanced requirements for those posing systemic risks. Penalties for non-compliance are severe, reaching up to €35 million or 7% of global annual revenue, depending on the violation type.

***

## FOUNDATIONAL DEFINITIONS

## What is an AI System?

Under Article 3 of the EU AI Act, an **AI system** is defined as:

> "A machine-based system that is designed to operate with varying levels of autonomy and that may exhibit adaptiveness after deployment, and that, for explicit or implicit objectives, infers, from the input it receives, how to generate outputs such as predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions that can influence physical or virtual environments."

This broad definition encompasses:

-   Machine learning systems
    
-   Deep learning models
    
-   Expert systems
    
-   Neural networks
    
-   Natural language processing systems
    
-   Computer vision systems
    
-   Recommendation algorithms
    
-   Predictive analytics tools
    
-   Generative AI models
    
-   Robotic systems with AI components
    

## What is a General Purpose AI (GPAI) Model?

According to Article 3(63), a **GPAI model** is:

> "An AI model that is trained with a large amount of data using self-supervision at scale, that displays significant generality and is capable of competently performing a wide range of distinct tasks regardless of the way the model is placed on the market, and that can be integrated into a variety of downstream systems or applications."

**Key characteristics of GPAI models:**

-   **Training compute threshold:** Models trained with greater than 10²³ FLOPs (floating-point operations) are indicatively considered GPAI models
    
-   **Functional generality:** Must demonstrate significant generality across a wide range of distinct tasks
    
-   **Modality support:** Typically capable of generating text, audio, images, or video
    
-   **Integration capability:** Can be incorporated into various downstream AI systems
    

**Exclusions from GPAI definition:**

-   Specialized models (transcription-only, image upscaling, weather forecasting, gaming AI)
    
-   Models used solely for research, development, or prototyping before market placement
    
-   Models lacking broad task competency

## RISK-BASED CLASSIFICATION SYSTEM

The EU AI Act's cornerstone is its **risk-based approach**, which assigns regulatory requirements proportional to the potential risks AI systems pose to health, safety, and fundamental rights.

## 1. UNACCEPTABLE RISK (PROHIBITED AI SYSTEMS)

**Status:** Outright banned in the European Union

**Timeline:** Prohibition effective February 2, 2025 (6 months after entry into force)

**Maximum penalty:** €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher

## Prohibited AI Practices

The Act identifies eight main categories of AI applications incompatible with EU values and fundamental rights:

## 1.1 Subliminal, Manipulative, or Deceptive Techniques

AI systems deploying subliminal, manipulative, or deceptive techniques to distort behavior and impair informed decision-making, causing significant harm to individuals.

**Examples:**

-   Voice-activated toys encouraging dangerous behavior in children
    
-   Subliminal messaging systems that operate below conscious awareness
    
-   AI systems designed to manipulate vulnerable individuals
    

## 1.2 Exploitation of Vulnerabilities

AI systems exploiting vulnerabilities related to:

-   Age (children, elderly)
    
-   Disability (physical or mental)
    
-   Socio-economic circumstances
    

Purpose: To distort behavior in ways causing significant harm.

## 1.3 Biometric Categorization Systems

AI systems inferring sensitive attributes from biometric data, including:

-   Race or ethnic origin
    
-   Political opinions
    
-   Trade union membership
    
-   Religious or philosophical beliefs
    
-   Sex life or sexual orientation
    

**Exceptions:**

-   Labeling or filtering of lawfully acquired biometric datasets for legitimate purposes
    
-   Law enforcement categorization of biometric data under specific legal conditions
    

## 1.4 Social Scoring Systems

AI systems that evaluate or classify individuals based on:

-   Social behavior
    
-   Personal characteristics
    
-   Predicted social behavior
    
-   Socio-economic status
    

Leading to:

-   Detrimental or unfavorable treatment
    
-   Treatment disproportionate to social behavior
    
-   Treatment unjustified by context
    

**Example:** Government-run social credit systems similar to those used in certain jurisdictions.

## 1.5 Facial Recognition Database Compilation

Compiling facial recognition databases through:

-   Untargeted scraping of facial images from the internet
    
-   Indiscriminate collection from CCTV footage
    
-   Other non-targeted collection methods
    

**Purpose of prohibition:** Prevents mass surveillance infrastructure and protects privacy rights.

## 1.6 Emotion Recognition in Workplace and Educational Institutions

AI systems inferring emotions in:

-   Workplaces
    
-   Educational institutions
    

**Exceptions:**

-   Medical or safety reasons (e.g., monitoring driver fatigue)
    
-   Healthcare diagnostic purposes
    

## 1.7 Real-Time Remote Biometric Identification (RBI) in Public Spaces

'Real-time' remote biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces for law enforcement purposes.

**Strictly limited exceptions for law enforcement:**

-   Searching for missing persons, abduction victims, human trafficking victims, or sexually exploited individuals
    
-   Preventing specific, substantial, and imminent threats to life or physical safety, or genuine and present threats of terrorist attacks
    
-   Identifying perpetrators or suspects of serious criminal offenses (specified list in Annex II)
    

**Additional safeguards for law enforcement exceptions:**

-   Must complete a **fundamental rights impact assessment** before deployment
    
-   Must **register the system in the EU database** (except in duly justified urgent cases)
    
-   Must obtain **authorization from a judicial authority or independent administrative authority** before deployment
    
-   In urgent cases, authorization must be requested within 24 hours; if rejected, deployment must cease immediately and all data deleted
    

## 1.8 Additional Prohibited Practices

-   Risk assessment systems predicting likelihood of criminal offense commission based solely on profiling or personality traits
    
-   Creation or expansion of facial recognition databases through untargeted scraping
    
-   Biometric categorization systems based on sensitive attributes in law enforcement (with narrow exceptions)

## 2. HIGH-RISK AI SYSTEMS

**Status:** Subject to stringent regulatory requirements

**Timeline:**

-   High-risk systems under Annex III: August 2, 2026 (24 months after entry into force)
    
-   High-risk systems under Annex I (safety components): August 2, 2027 (36 months)
    

**Maximum penalty for non-compliance:** €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover

## 2.1 Classification Criteria

An AI system is classified as high-risk if it meets one of two conditions:

**Condition A: Safety Component of Regulated Products (Article 6(1) - Annex I)**

The AI system is:

-   Intended to be used as a safety component of a product, OR
    
-   The AI system itself is a product
    

Covered by EU harmonization legislation listed in Annex I, including:

-   Machinery
    
-   Toys
    
-   Medical devices
    
-   In vitro diagnostic medical devices
    
-   Radio equipment
    
-   Aviation safety
    
-   Automotive vehicles
    
-   Agricultural and forestry vehicles
    
-   Marine equipment
    
-   Rail systems
    
-   Civil explosives
    
-   Equipment for use in potentially explosive atmospheres
    
-   Personal protective equipment
    
-   Cableway installations
    
-   Appliances burning gaseous fuels
    
-   Lifts
    

**AND** the product must undergo third-party conformity assessment.

**Condition B: Stand-Alone AI Systems in Specific Areas (Article 6(2) - Annex III)**

AI systems listed in Annex III are always considered high-risk unless they qualify for exemptions (see Section 2.3).

## 2.2 High-Risk Categories Under Annex III

**Category 1: Biometrics**

-   Remote biometric identification systems (excluding real-time in public spaces, which is prohibited)
    
-   Biometric verification and identification systems
    
-   Emotion recognition systems
    

**Category 2: Critical Infrastructure**

-   AI systems intended to be used as safety components in the management and operation of:
    
    -   Road traffic
        
    -   Water supply
        
    -   Gas supply
        
    -   Heating supply
        
    -   Electricity supply
        
    -   Digital infrastructure
        

**Category 3: Education and Vocational Training**

-   AI systems determining access to educational institutions or admission
    
-   AI systems assessing students in educational settings
    
-   AI systems evaluating learning outcomes
    
-   AI systems monitoring and detecting prohibited behavior during tests
    
-   AI systems allocating students to educational programs
    

**Category 4: Employment, Workers Management, and Access to Self-Employment**

-   AI systems for recruitment or selection of natural persons (e.g., CV-scanning tools, video interview analyzers)
    
-   AI systems for making decisions affecting terms of work-related relationships
    
-   AI systems for promotion and termination decisions
    
-   AI systems for task allocation
    
-   AI systems monitoring and evaluating performance and behavior of workers
    

**Category 5: Access to Essential Private and Public Services and Benefits**

-   AI systems evaluating eligibility for public assistance benefits and services
    
-   AI systems evaluating creditworthiness (except fraud detection)
    
-   AI systems dispatching or establishing priority in emergency first response services
    
-   AI systems assessing and classifying emergency calls
    

**Category 6: Law Enforcement**

-   AI systems assessing risk of individuals becoming victims of criminal offenses
    
-   AI systems as polygraphs or similar tools
    
-   AI systems evaluating reliability of evidence in criminal proceedings
    
-   AI systems assessing risk of offending or reoffending
    
-   AI systems profiling individuals during detection, investigation, or prosecution of criminal offenses
    

**Category 7: Migration, Asylum, and Border Control Management**

-   AI systems as polygraphs or similar tools for border control
    
-   AI systems assessing risk posed by individuals intending to enter or having entered the territory
    
-   AI systems examining applications for asylum, visa, and residence permits
    
-   AI systems detecting, recognizing, or identifying natural persons (with exceptions for verification)
    

**Category 8: Administration of Justice and Democratic Processes**

-   AI systems assisting judicial authorities in researching and interpreting facts and law
    
-   AI systems applying law to concrete sets of facts
    
-   AI systems influencing election or referendum outcomes
    
-   AI systems influencing voter behavior
    

## 2.3 Exemptions from High-Risk Classification

AI systems listed in Annex III will NOT be considered high-risk if they do not pose a significant risk of harm to health, safety, or fundamental rights, including by not materially influencing decision-making outcomes.

**Exemption conditions (at least one must be met):**

**A. Narrow Procedural Task**  
The AI system performs a narrow procedural task, such as:

-   Formatting data
    
-   Organizing information
    
-   Administrative functions
    

**B. Improvement of Prior Human Activity**  
The AI system improves the result of a previously completed human activity without replacing human judgment.

**C. Pattern Detection Without Replacement**  
The AI system:

-   Detects decision-making patterns or deviations from prior patterns
    
-   Is not meant to replace or influence previously completed human assessments
    
-   Requires proper human review before decisions are made
    

**D. Preparatory Task Only**  
The AI system performs only a preparatory task to an assessment (e.g., data collection, preliminary analysis).

**Important note on profiling:** AI systems listed in Annex III are ALWAYS considered high-risk if they profile individuals—defined as automated processing of personal data to assess various aspects of a person's life, including work performance, economic situation, health, preferences, interests, reliability, behavior, location, or movements.

## 2.4 Requirements for High-Risk AI Systems

Providers of high-risk AI systems must comply with extensive obligations:

**A. Risk Management System (Article 9)**

-   Establish and maintain a continuous risk management system throughout the AI system's entire lifecycle
    
-   Conduct risk identification, analysis, estimation, and evaluation
    
-   Implement risk mitigation measures
    
-   Provide information to users about residual risks
    
-   Test risk management measures
    
-   Review and update the system regularly, especially after serious incidents
    

**B. Data Governance (Article 10)**

-   Ensure training, validation, and testing datasets are:
    
    -   Relevant to the intended purpose
        
    -   Sufficiently representative
        
    -   Free of errors to the best extent possible
        
    -   Complete for their intended purpose
        
-   Apply appropriate data governance and management practices
    
-   Consider:
    
    -   Design choices
        
    -   Data collection processes
        
    -   Data preparation operations (cleaning, labeling, aggregation)
        
    -   Formulation of assumptions
        
    -   Assessment of data availability, quantity, and suitability
        
-   For biometric identification and categorization: special attention to ensure datasets are representative of various geographic, behavioral, and functional settings
    

**C. Technical Documentation (Article 11 & Annex IV)**  
Draw up and maintain comprehensive technical documentation demonstrating compliance, including:

-   General description of the AI system
    
-   Detailed description of system elements and development process
    
-   Monitoring, functioning, and control mechanisms
    
-   Risk management information
    
-   Changes made to the system throughout its lifecycle
    
-   List of harmonized standards or technical specifications applied
    
-   Description of datasets used (training, validation, testing)
    
-   Assessment of human oversight measures
    
-   Cybersecurity measures
    

**D. Record-Keeping and Logging (Article 12)**

-   Design AI systems to automatically record events (logs) throughout their lifetime
    
-   Logs must enable:
    
    -   Traceability of the system's functioning
        
    -   Monitoring throughout the system's lifecycle
        
    -   Post-market monitoring
        
-   Logging capabilities must be commensurate with the intended purpose and risk level
    
-   Logs must be protected against tampering or unauthorized access
    

**E. Transparency and User Information (Article 13)**

-   Design and develop systems ensuring sufficiently transparent operation
    
-   Provide users with information enabling them to:
    
    -   Interpret the system's output
        
    -   Use the system appropriately
        
-   Documentation and instructions for use must be:
    
    -   Concise, complete, correct, and clear
        
    -   Relevant, accessible, and comprehensible to users
        
    -   In an appropriate digital or non-digital format
        

**F. Human Oversight (Article 14)**

-   Design AI systems to enable effective human oversight
    
-   Measures must enable the person to whom oversight is assigned to:
    
    -   Fully understand the AI system's capabilities and limitations
        
    -   Remain aware of automation bias
        
    -   Correctly interpret the system's output
        
    -   Decide not to use the system or disregard, override, or reverse its output
        
    -   Intervene or interrupt the system's operation
        
-   Human oversight must be ensured through either:
    
    -   Measures integrated into the AI system, OR
        
    -   Measures identified by the provider and implemented by the user
        

**G. Accuracy, Robustness, and Cybersecurity (Article 15)**

-   Design and develop systems to achieve appropriate levels of:
    
    -   **Accuracy:** Ability to produce correct outputs
        
    -   **Robustness:** Ability to function reliably under various conditions
        
    -   **Cybersecurity:** Protection against vulnerabilities, attacks, and unauthorized access
        
-   Systems must perform consistently throughout their lifecycle
    
-   Systems must be resilient against:
    
    -   Errors, faults, or inconsistencies
        
    -   Attempts to manipulate the system or its outputs
        
-   Technical and organizational cybersecurity measures must be implemented
    

**H. Quality Management System (Article 17)**  
Establish, implement, document, and maintain a quality management system ensuring compliance with the Act, including:

-   Strategy for regulatory compliance
    
-   Design, quality control, and quality assurance techniques
    
-   Post-market monitoring procedures
    
-   Procedures for data management
    
-   Risk management system
    
-   Technical documentation procedures
    
-   Incident reporting procedures
    
-   Communication with authorities
    
-   Systems for record-keeping
    
-   Resource management
    
-   Accountability framework
    

**I. Conformity Assessment (Article 43)**

-   Undergo conformity assessment before placing on the market or putting into service
    
-   Assessment may be conducted by:
    
    -   Provider (self-assessment) for certain systems
        
    -   Notified bodies (independent third parties) for higher-risk systems
        
-   Affix CE marking upon successful conformity assessment
    

**J. EU Database Registration (Article 49)**

-   Register the system in the EU-wide public database before placing on the market
    
-   Provide required information including:
    
    -   Provider identification
        
    -   System description
        
    -   Intended purpose
        
    -   Risk category
        
    -   CE marking information
        

**K. Post-Market Monitoring (Article 72)**

-   Establish and document a post-market monitoring system
    
-   Actively and systematically collect, document, and analyze data on the performance of high-risk AI systems throughout their lifetime
    
-   Report serious incidents to market surveillance authorities
    
-   Cooperate with authorities during investigations
    

**L. Serious Incident Reporting (Article 73)**  
Providers must report serious incidents to market surveillance authorities without undue delay.

**Serious incidents** are defined as AI system malfunctions or incidents leading to:

-   Death or serious harm to a person's health
    
-   Serious and irreversible disruption to critical infrastructure
    
-   Breaches of obligations protecting fundamental rights
    
-   Serious harm to property or the environment

## 3. GENERAL PURPOSE AI (GPAI) MODELS

**Status:** Specific obligations framework separate from risk tiers

**Timeline:**

-   GPAI obligations effective: August 2, 2025 (12 months after entry into force)
    
-   Full enforcement powers: August 2, 2026
    
-   Legacy GPAI models (placed on market before August 2, 2025): Must comply by August 2, 2027
    

**Maximum penalty:** €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover

## 3.1 Determining GPAI Model Status

**Indicative threshold for GPAI models:**

-   **Training compute:** Greater than 10²³ FLOPs (floating-point operations)
    
-   **Parameter guidance:** Models with approximately 1 billion parameters trained on substantial datasets typically meet this threshold
    
-   **Functional requirement:** Must display significant generality across a wide range of distinct tasks
    
-   **Modality:** Capable of generating text, audio, images, or video
    

**Non-GPAI specialized models (excluded):**

-   Transcription-only systems
    
-   Image upscaling tools
    
-   Weather forecasting models
    
-   Gaming AI systems
    
-   Other task-specific models lacking broad generality
    

## 3.2 GPAI Provider Definition

**Who is considered a GPAI provider:**

-   Original developers of GPAI models
    
-   Organizations that significantly modify existing GPAI models
    

**Modification threshold for becoming a provider:**  
Using compute resources equivalent to **one-third of the original model's training compute** (or one-third of 10²³ FLOPs if original compute unknown).

**Not considered providers:**

-   Fine-tuners using less than one-third threshold
    
-   Deployers integrating GPAI into downstream systems without substantial modification
    
-   Users of GPAI models
    

## 3.3 Standard GPAI Obligations (All GPAI Providers)

**A. Technical Documentation (Article 53(1)(a))**  
Draw up and keep up-to-date technical documentation, including:

-   Model architecture and design
    
-   Model parameters and size
    
-   Input and output modalities
    
-   Training process details
    
-   Computational resources used for training
    
-   Energy consumption during training
    
-   Datasets used (types and sources)
    
-   Testing and evaluation procedures
    
-   Evaluation results
    

**B. Information for Downstream Providers (Article 53(1)(b))**  
Draw up, keep up-to-date, and make available documentation for downstream providers integrating the GPAI model, enabling them to:

-   Understand the model's capabilities and limitations
    
-   Comply with their own obligations under the Act
    
-   Use the model appropriately
    

Information must include:

-   Intended uses and known limitations
    
-   Model performance characteristics
    
-   Technical specifications
    
-   Integration guidance
    
-   Safety and security considerations
    

**C. Copyright Compliance Policy (Article 53(1)(c))**  
Establish and maintain a policy to respect Union copyright law, particularly:

-   Directive (EU) 2019/790 (Copyright Directive)
    
-   Ensure training data complies with copyright obligations
    
-   Document copyright compliance measures
    

**D. Public Training Content Summary (Article 53(1)(d))**  
Publish a sufficiently detailed summary of the content used for training the GPAI model.

**Requirements:**

-   Must use template provided by the AI Office
    
-   Must be publicly accessible
    
-   Must include:
    
    -   General description of data sources
        
    -   Types of content used
        
    -   Identification of public vs. private datasets
        
    -   Data from web scraping
        
    -   User-generated data
        
-   Published on a publicly accessible website or through the EU database
    

**Timeline for template:** AI Office to provide template by specific date (being finalized in 2025).

## 3.4 GPAI Models with Systemic Risk

**Definition of systemic risk:**  
GPAI models are presumed to have systemic risk if they have:

-   **High-impact capabilities:** Assessed through appropriate technical tools and methodologies
    
-   **Cumulative amount of compute:** Used for training measured in floating-point operations ≥ **10²⁵ FLOPs**
    
-   **Commission designation:** The European Commission may designate models as systemic risk through implementing acts
    

**Assessment factors:**

-   Number of parameters
    
-   Model size and complexity
    
-   Quality and quantity of training data
    
-   Modalities (text, image, video, audio)
    
-   Output quality and sophistication
    
-   Market reach and number of users
    
-   Potential impact on internal market
    
-   Potential societal impact
    

**Systemic risk examples:**

-   Large language models (LLMs) with hundreds of billions of parameters
    
-   Multimodal foundation models with broad capabilities
    
-   Models widely deployed across critical sectors
    

## 3.5 Additional Obligations for GPAI Models with Systemic Risk

In addition to the four standard GPAI obligations, providers of systemic risk models must:

**E. Model Evaluation (Article 55(1)(a))**  
Perform model evaluations, including:

-   **Adversarial testing:** Conducting and documenting adversarial testing of the model to identify and mitigate systemic risks
    
-   Testing for potential harms, including:
    
    -   Generation of harmful content
        
    -   Cybersecurity vulnerabilities
        
    -   Misuse for illegal purposes
        
    -   Risks to public safety
        
    -   Impacts on fundamental rights
        
-   Using appropriate evaluation methodologies and benchmarks
    
-   Documenting evaluation procedures and results
    

**F. Systemic Risk Assessment and Mitigation (Article 55(1)(b))**

-   Assess and mitigate possible systemic risks at Union level
    
-   Consider sources of systemic risk, including:
    
    -   Accidents at large scale
        
    -   Misuse for illegal purposes
        
    -   Cyber attacks
        
    -   Impact on democratic processes
        
    -   Public safety risks
        
    -   Negative effects on fundamental rights
        
-   Implement measures to address identified risks
    
-   Document risk assessments and mitigation strategies
    
-   Establish a "state of the art safety and security framework"
    
-   Define responsibilities for systemic risk management
    
-   Assign adequate resources
    

**G. Serious Incident Tracking and Reporting (Article 55(1)(c))**

-   Track, document, and report serious incidents and possible corrective measures
    
-   Report to:
    
    -   The AI Office (European Commission)
        
    -   Relevant national competent authorities
        
-   Report without undue delay after incident identification
    
-   Document incident details, impacts, and remedial actions taken
    

**H. Cybersecurity Protection (Article 55(1)(d))**

-   Ensure an adequate level of cybersecurity protection for:
    
    -   The GPAI model
        
    -   The physical infrastructure
        
    -   Training data
        
    -   Model weights and parameters
        
    -   Inference systems
        
-   Implement technical and organizational security measures
    
-   Protect against:
    
    -   Unauthorized access
        
    -   Model theft
        
    -   Data breaches
        
    -   Adversarial attacks
        
    -   Model poisoning
        

## 3.6 GPAI Code of Practice

**Status:** Published July 10, 2025; formally approved August 1, 2025

**Purpose:**

-   Voluntary framework for demonstrating compliance with GPAI obligations
    
-   Developed through multi-stakeholder process led by the AI Office
    
-   Signatories can rely on adherence to demonstrate Act compliance
    

**Coverage areas:**

-   Transparency requirements
    
-   Copyright compliance measures
    
-   Safety and security frameworks
    
-   Risk management procedures
    
-   Documentation standards
    
-   Incident reporting protocols
    

**Non-mandatory nature:**

-   Providers may choose alternative compliance methods
    
-   However, the Code will likely shape regulatory expectations
    
-   Non-signatories must demonstrate compliance through other means
    

**Alternative if Code inadequate:**  
If the AI Office deems the Code inadequate, the European Commission may provide common rules through implementing acts.

## 4. LIMITED RISK AI SYSTEMS

**Status:** Subject to transparency obligations

**Timeline:** Applies from general application date (August 2, 2026)

**Maximum penalty for non-compliance:** €7.5 million or 1.5% of global annual turnover

## 4.1 Definition and Scope

Limited risk AI systems are those that pose a risk of manipulation or deceit but do not qualify as high-risk or prohibited.

**Examples:**

-   Chatbots and conversational AI
    
-   Generative AI systems creating content
    
-   Emotion recognition systems (outside workplace/education contexts)
    
-   Biometric categorization systems (outside prohibited contexts)
    
-   AI systems generating or manipulating image, audio, or video content (deepfakes)
    

## 4.2 Transparency Requirements (Article 50)

**A. Interaction with AI Systems**  
When interacting with an AI system, providers and deployers must ensure that natural persons are informed that they are interacting with an AI system, unless this is obvious from the circumstances and context of use.

**Application:**

-   Chatbots must disclose their AI nature
    
-   Virtual assistants must be clearly identified as AI
    
-   Customer service bots must inform users
    
-   Applies to both text and voice interactions
    

**Exception:** Disclosure not required when it is obvious from the circumstances that the user is interacting with AI.

**B. Emotion Recognition Systems**  
Deployers of emotion recognition systems must inform natural persons exposed to the system that they are being subjected to such a system.

**Requirement details:**

-   Clear notification before emotion recognition begins
    
-   Explanation of the system's purpose
    
-   Information about data processing
    
-   Applies to all emotion recognition not covered by workplace/education prohibition
    

**C. Biometric Categorization Systems**  
Deployers of biometric categorization systems must inform natural persons exposed to the system.

**Requirements:**

-   Notice must be provided before categorization occurs
    
-   Information about categories being inferred
    
-   Transparency about biometric data processing
    

**D. AI-Generated Content (Deepfakes)**  
AI systems generating or manipulating:

-   Text content published to inform the public on matters of public interest
    
-   Image, audio, or video content (deepfakes)
    

Must be clearly labeled as artificially generated or manipulated.

**Specific requirements:**

**For text content:**

-   Clear disclosure when AI-generated text is published
    
-   Applicable when content relates to matters of public interest
    
-   Disclosure must be prominent and easily noticeable
    

**For image, audio, and video content (deepfakes):**

-   **Machine-readable disclosure:** Content must include machine-detectable markers indicating artificial generation
    
-   **Human-perceptible disclosure:** Content must be marked in a way perceptible to users (watermarks, labels, notices)
    
-   Requirements apply to:
    
    -   AI-generated synthetic content
        
    -   AI-manipulated authentic content creating false impressions
        
-   Special consideration for creative, artistic, or fictional content
    

**Exceptions from deepfake labeling:**

-   Content produced as part of creative, satirical, artistic, or fictional work
    
-   Content where disclosure would be inappropriate given the nature of the work
    
-   Content authorized by law for law enforcement, judicial proceedings, or national security

## 5. MINIMAL OR NO RISK AI SYSTEMS

**Status:** No specific regulatory obligations; voluntary compliance encouraged

**Timeline:** No mandatory compliance requirements

**Penalties:** Not applicable

## 5.1 Definition and Scope

AI systems posing minimal or no risk to health, safety, or fundamental rights are left largely unregulated under the Act.

**Examples:**

-   AI-enabled video games
    
-   Spam filters
    
-   Inventory management systems
    
-   Product recommendation systems (non-sensitive)
    
-   AI-enhanced photo editing tools
    
-   Personal productivity applications
    
-   Entertainment AI systems
    
-   Basic automation tools
    

## 5.2 Voluntary Codes of Conduct

Providers of minimal risk AI systems are encouraged to:

-   Adhere to voluntary codes of conduct
    
-   Adopt best practices for ethical AI development
    
-   Implement transparency measures
    
-   Consider environmental sustainability
    
-   Respect user privacy
    
-   Follow industry standards
    

**Benefits of voluntary compliance:**

-   Builds consumer trust
    
-   Demonstrates commitment to responsible AI
    
-   Prepares for potential future regulation
    
-   Aligns with ethical AI principles
    
-   Competitive advantage through trustworthiness

## ROLES AND RESPONSIBILITIES

The EU AI Act defines specific obligations for different actors in the AI value chain.

## Providers

**Definition:** Any natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or other body that develops an AI system or GPAI model or that has an AI system or GPAI model developed and places it on the market or puts it into service under its own name or trademark, whether for payment or free of charge.

**Primary responsibilities:**

-   Ensure AI systems comply with requirements before placing on market
    
-   Establish and maintain quality management systems
    
-   Draw up technical documentation
    
-   Maintain logs automatically generated by AI systems
    
-   Conduct conformity assessments
    
-   Register high-risk AI systems in EU database
    
-   Affix CE marking
    
-   Implement post-market monitoring
    
-   Report serious incidents
    
-   Cooperate with competent authorities
    
-   Take corrective action when systems are non-compliant
    

**Geographic scope:** Includes providers located outside the EU if:

-   AI system's output is used in the EU
    
-   AI system is placed on the EU market
    

## Deployers

**Definition:** Any natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or other body using an AI system under its authority, except where the AI system is used in the course of a personal non-professional activity.

**Primary responsibilities:**

-   Use AI systems according to instructions for use
    
-   Ensure human oversight measures are implemented
    
-   Monitor AI system operation for risks
    
-   Inform providers of serious incidents or malfunctions
    
-   Keep logs generated by high-risk AI systems
    
-   Conduct data protection impact assessments when required
    
-   For high-risk systems:
    
    -   Ensure input data is relevant and representative
        
    -   Suspend use if serious incidents occur
        
    -   Inform affected persons about use of high-risk AI
        

**Becoming a provider:** Deployers become providers if they:

-   Modify a high-risk AI system's intended purpose
    
-   Make substantial modifications to a high-risk AI system
    

## Distributors

**Definition:** Any natural or legal person in the supply chain, other than the provider or importer, that makes an AI system available on the Union market.

**Primary responsibilities:**

-   Verify that high-risk AI systems bear CE marking
    
-   Verify that technical documentation and instructions for use are provided
    
-   Inform providers or importers about non-compliant AI systems
    
-   Cooperate with competent authorities
    
-   Ensure storage and transport conditions preserve compliance
    

**Becoming a provider:** Distributors become providers if they:

-   Place an AI system on the market under their own name or trademark
    
-   Modify an AI system already on the market
    

## Importers

**Definition:** Any natural or legal person located or established in the Union that places on the market an AI system that bears the name or trademark of a natural or legal person established outside the Union.

**Primary responsibilities:**

-   Verify that appropriate conformity assessment has been carried out
    
-   Verify that technical documentation is available
    
-   Verify that AI system bears CE marking
    
-   Verify that instructions for use are provided
    
-   Verify that provider has designated an authorized representative (if outside EU)
    
-   Register high-risk AI systems in EU database if provider fails to do so
    
-   Ensure storage and transport conditions preserve compliance
    
-   Cooperate with market surveillance authorities
    

## Authorized Representatives

**Definition:** Any natural or legal person located or established in the Union who has received and accepted a written mandate from a provider of an AI system to act on its behalf.

**Responsibilities:**

-   Verify that EU declaration of conformity and technical documentation are drawn up
    
-   Keep copy of technical documentation available for authorities
    
-   Cooperate with competent authorities
    
-   Provide authorities with information upon request
    
-   Respond to requests from authorities concerning AI system compliance
    

**Required when:** Provider is located outside the European Union.

## GOVERNANCE AND ENFORCEMENT STRUCTURE

The EU AI Act establishes a comprehensive multi-level governance framework combining European and national authorities.

## European Level Governance

## European AI Office (Article 64)

**Status:** Operational since February 21, 2024

**Location:** Established within the European Commission

**Primary functions:**

-   **Oversight of GPAI models:** Supervises providers of general-purpose AI models, especially those with systemic risk
    
-   **Regulatory development:** Drafts delegated acts, implementing acts, and guidelines
    
-   **Database management:** Manages the EU-wide database for high-risk AI systems
    
-   **International coordination:** Represents the EU in international AI governance forums
    
-   **Code of practice facilitation:** Coordinates development of GPAI codes of practice
    
-   **Enforcement:** Exercises market surveillance authority powers for GPAI models
    
-   **Information requests:** Can request documentation, evaluations, and other information from GPAI providers
    
-   **Corrective measures:** Can order model recalls, mandate mitigations, or impose restrictions
    
-   **Penalties:** Can impose fines for non-compliance
    

**Enforcement timeline:**

-   Informal cooperation and guidance: From August 2, 2025
    
-   Full enforcement powers (including fines): From August 2, 2026
    

## European Artificial Intelligence Board (Articles 65-66)

**Composition:** One representative from each EU Member State

**Status:** First meeting convened on August 2, 2025

**Functions:**

-   Advise and assist the Commission on AI Act implementation
    
-   Coordinate between national competent authorities
    
-   Collect and share expertise, best practices, and data
    
-   Contribute to harmonized administrative practices
    
-   Issue recommendations, opinions, and advice
    
-   Facilitate cooperation between market surveillance authorities
    

**Working arrangements:**

-   Meets regularly as convened by the Commission
    
-   May establish sub-groups on specific topics
    
-   Provides forum for Member State coordination
    

## Scientific Panel of Independent Experts (Article 68)

**Composition:** Independent experts selected based on scientific and technical expertise in AI

**Functions:**

-   Support the AI Office and Commission with scientific advice
    
-   Provide expertise on:
    
    -   GPAI model evaluation methodologies
        
    -   Systemic risk assessment
        
    -   Technical standards
        
    -   State-of-the-art AI developments
        
-   Assist in developing testing and evaluation protocols
    
-   Advise on classification of AI systems
    
-   Support with technical guidance and recommendations
    

**Independence:** Members act in public interest, not representing any organization or Member State.

## Advisory Forum (Article 67)

**Composition:** Diverse stakeholder representation, including:

-   Industry representatives
    
-   Start-ups and SMEs
    
-   Civil society organizations
    
-   Academic and research institutions
    
-   Trade unions
    
-   Consumer organizations
    
-   Other relevant stakeholders
    

**Functions:**

-   Provide technical expertise to the Board and Commission
    
-   Represent commercial and non-commercial stakeholder perspectives
    
-   Contribute to development of codes of practice
    
-   Advise on implementation challenges and opportunities
    
-   Support balanced approach to AI regulation
    

**Structure:** Ensures multistakeholder governance and inclusive policymaking.

## National Level Governance

## Market Surveillance Authorities (Article 74)

**Designation deadline:** Each Member State must designate by August 2, 2025

**Number:** At least one per Member State (multiple authorities may be designated)

**Functions:**

-   **Supervision:** Monitor AI systems placed on national markets
    
-   **Investigation:** Conduct investigations into potential non-compliance
    
-   **Enforcement:** Take corrective actions against non-compliant systems
    
-   **Orders:** Can mandate:
    
    -   Withdrawal of AI systems from the market
        
    -   Prohibition of making systems available
        
    -   Recall of systems already in use
        
    -   Updates or modifications to systems
        
    -   Suspension of system operation
        
-   **Penalties:** Impose administrative fines for violations
    
-   **Cooperation:** Work with market surveillance authorities in other Member States
    
-   **Reporting:** Report serious incidents to Commission and other authorities
    
-   **Database access:** Access EU database for high-risk AI systems
    

**Authority requirements:**

-   Adequate technical, financial, and human resources
    
-   Independence, impartiality, and absence of bias
    
-   Infrastructure to effectively execute tasks
    

## Notifying Authorities (Article 28)

**Designation deadline:** Each Member State must designate by August 2, 2025

**Definition:** Public body (ministry, accreditation agency, or similar) responsible for notified bodies

**Functions:**

-   **Designation:** Evaluate, designate, and notify conformity assessment bodies (notified bodies)
    
-   **Monitoring:** Supervise notified bodies' activities and ensure continued compliance
    
-   **Suspension/withdrawal:** Can suspend or withdraw notified body designation if requirements not met
    
-   **Notification to Commission:** Inform Commission and other Member States of designated notified bodies
    
-   **Quality assurance:** Ensure notified bodies maintain competence and capability
    

**Requirements:**

-   Organizational independence from conformity assessment bodies
    
-   Impartiality in performing tasks
    
-   No conflicts of interest
    

## Notified Bodies (Article 33)

**Status:** Independent third-party conformity assessment organizations

**Selection:** Designated by notifying authorities based on competence assessment

**Functions:**

-   **Conformity assessment:** Conduct independent third-party assessment of high-risk AI systems
    
-   **Testing and evaluation:** Test AI systems against regulatory requirements
    
-   **Technical file review:** Examine technical documentation for compliance
    
-   **Quality system audit:** Assess provider quality management systems
    
-   **Certificates:** Issue EU technical documentation assessment certificates
    
-   **Surveillance:** Conduct periodic surveillance of certified systems
    
-   **Reporting:** Report concerns to market surveillance authorities
    

**Requirements:**

-   Demonstrated technical competence in AI and relevant domains
    
-   Independence and impartiality
    
-   Adequate resources and equipment
    
-   Procedures ensuring confidentiality
    
-   Professional liability insurance
    

## Fundamental Rights Protection Authorities

**Designation deadline:** Each Member State must identify and publish list by November 2, 2024

**Definition:** Public authorities or bodies empowered to supervise or enforce respect of obligations under Union law protecting fundamental rights.

**Examples:**

-   Data protection authorities
    
-   Equality bodies
    
-   Human rights commissions
    
-   Ombudsperson offices
    

**Special rights under the AI Act:**

-   **Right to information:** Must be informed by market surveillance authorities about serious incidents, investigations, and non-compliance findings
    
-   **Right to request information:** Can request information and cooperation from market surveillance authorities
    
-   **Right to request provider cooperation:** Can request cooperation from providers of high-risk AI systems involved in incidents
    
-   **Right to participate:** Can engage in oversight activities related to fundamental rights impacts
    

**Objective:** Ensure high-risk AI systems comply with fundamental rights obligations.

**France example:** Three designated authorities:

-   DGCCRF (Directorate General for Competition Policy, Consumer Affairs and Fraud Control)
    
-   CNIL (National Commission on Informatics and Liberty)
    
-   Defender of Rights
    

**Variation across Member States:**

-   Some Member States: One authority (e.g., Latvia)
    
-   Some Member States: Many authorities (e.g., Ireland with 9, Portugal with 14)
    

## Single Point of Contact (Article 70)

**Designation:** One national competent authority designated as single point of contact (typically market surveillance authority)

**Function:**

-   Central coordination with the Commission
    
-   Central coordination with other Member States
    
-   Facilitate cross-border cooperation
    
-   Streamline communication
    

## European Data Protection Supervisor (EDPS)

**Scope:** AI systems used or deployed by EU institutions, agencies, offices, and bodies

**Dual role:**

-   Market surveillance authority for EU institutions' AI systems
    
-   Notifying authority for EU institutions

## COMPLIANCE OBLIGATIONS BY ROLE

## For All Providers

**Pre-Market:**

1.  Classify AI system into appropriate risk category
    
2.  Determine applicable requirements based on classification
    
3.  Conduct risk assessment
    
4.  Implement required technical and organizational measures
    
5.  Prepare technical documentation
    
6.  Establish quality management system (if high-risk)
    
7.  Conduct conformity assessment (if high-risk)
    
8.  Register in EU database (if high-risk)
    
9.  Affix CE marking (if high-risk)
    
10.  Prepare instructions for use
     

**Post-Market:**

1.  Implement post-market monitoring system
    
2.  Track and respond to incidents
    
3.  Report serious incidents to authorities
    
4.  Maintain and update technical documentation
    
5.  Maintain and update quality management system
    
6.  Respond to authority requests for information
    
7.  Take corrective action when non-compliance identified
    
8.  Update registration in EU database as needed
    
9.  Cooperate with market surveillance investigations
    

## For GPAI Providers (Standard)

1.  Draw up technical documentation
    
2.  Create and maintain downstream provider information package
    
3.  Establish copyright compliance policy
    
4.  Publish training content summary using AI Office template
    
5.  Make documentation available to:
    
    -   Downstream providers
        
    -   AI Office (upon request)
        
    -   National authorities (upon request)
        

## For GPAI Providers (Systemic Risk)

**All standard GPAI obligations PLUS:**

1.  Perform model evaluations including adversarial testing
    
2.  Assess and mitigate systemic risks
    
3.  Track, document, and report serious incidents to AI Office and national authorities
    
4.  Ensure adequate cybersecurity protection
    
5.  Establish state-of-the-art safety and security framework
    
6.  Notify Commission if model meets systemic risk criteria
    
7.  Cooperate with AI Office on risk assessments
    

## For Deployers of High-Risk AI Systems

**Before Deployment:**

1.  Read and understand instructions for use
    
2.  Ensure input data is relevant and sufficiently representative
    
3.  Conduct fundamental rights impact assessment (if applicable)
    
4.  Conduct data protection impact assessment (if processing personal data)
    
5.  Ensure human oversight measures are in place
    
6.  Designate personnel responsible for oversight
    
7.  Inform affected persons about AI system use (if applicable)
    

**During Operation:**

1.  Use system according to instructions
    
2.  Implement human oversight
    
3.  Monitor system for anomalies, malfunctions, or unexpected performance
    
4.  Maintain logs generated by the system
    
5.  Ensure data used remains relevant and representative
    
6.  Regularly review system performance
    

**Incident Management:**

1.  Suspend use if serious incident occurs
    
2.  Inform provider about incident without undue delay
    
3.  Inform market surveillance authority if provider does not act
    
4.  Cooperate with investigations
    
5.  Maintain records of incidents and actions taken

## PENALTIES AND ENFORCEMENT

The EU AI Act establishes a harmonized penalties framework with severe financial consequences for non-compliance.

## Administrative Fines Framework

Penalties are tiered based on violation severity:

## Tier 1: Prohibited AI Systems

**Maximum fine:** €35 million OR 7% of total worldwide annual turnover of the preceding financial year, **whichever is higher**

**Violations:**

-   Placing prohibited AI system on the market, putting into service, or using (Article 5)
    
-   Examples:
    
    -   Deploying social scoring systems
        
    -   Using manipulative AI techniques
        
    -   Conducting real-time biometric identification in public spaces without authorization
        
    -   Creating untargeted facial recognition databases
        

## Tier 2: Other Act Obligations

**Maximum fine:** €15 million OR 3% of total worldwide annual turnover, **whichever is higher**

**Violations:**

-   Non-compliance with high-risk AI system requirements (Chapter III, Section 2)
    
-   Non-compliance with GPAI obligations for models with systemic risk (Article 55)
    
-   Violating obligations of notified bodies (Article 31)
    
-   Examples:
    
    -   Failing to conduct required conformity assessment
        
    -   Not implementing risk management system
        
    -   Inadequate data governance
        
    -   Failing to register in EU database
        
    -   Insufficient post-market monitoring
        

## Tier 3: Information and Documentation

**Maximum fine:** €7.5 million OR 1.5% of total worldwide annual turnover, **whichever is higher**

**Violations:**

-   Supplying incorrect, incomplete, or misleading information to authorities (Article 78)
    
-   Failing to respond to authority requests
    
-   Providing false documentation
    

## Tier 4: Other Provisions

**Maximum fine:** €7.5 million OR 1.5% of total worldwide annual turnover, **whichever is higher**

**Violations:**

-   Non-compliance with other provisions of the Act
    
-   Transparency obligation violations (Article 50)
    
-   Limited risk AI system requirements
    
-   Examples:
    
    -   Failing to disclose chatbot nature to users
        
    -   Not labeling AI-generated content
        
    -   Missing emotion recognition disclosures
        

## Special Provisions

## SMEs and Start-ups

**Definition of SME:** Micro, small, and medium-sized enterprises as defined under Commission Recommendation 2003/361/EC:

-   Micro: < 10 employees, turnover/balance sheet ≤ €2 million
    
-   Small: < 50 employees, turnover/balance sheet ≤ €10 million
    
-   Medium: < 250 employees, turnover ≤ €50 million, balance sheet ≤ €43 million
    

**Penalty adjustments:**

-   Fines for SMEs and start-ups shall be proportionate to their size and market position
    
-   Lower financial thresholds may apply
    
-   Consideration of economic circumstances
    
-   Aim: Avoid disproportionate impact while ensuring compliance
    

**Compliance support:**

-   Regulatory sandboxes available
    
-   Leniency in documentation requirements
    
-   Technical support from authorities
    
-   Guidance and templates provided
    
-   Dedicated helpdesks in Member States
    

## Natural Persons

Fines imposed on natural persons shall take into account their economic situation.

## Enforcement Measures Beyond Fines

Market surveillance authorities have additional enforcement tools:

**Corrective Measures:**

-   **Product withdrawal:** Order removal from market
    
-   **Product recall:** Require recall of products already distributed
    
-   **Prohibition:** Ban making AI system available on market
    
-   **Restriction:** Limit use to specific conditions or users
    
-   **Suspension:** Temporarily halt operation pending investigation
    
-   **Modification orders:** Require updates, patches, or changes to system
    

**Provisional Measures:**  
When serious and immediate risk identified:

-   Immediately restrict or prohibit making AI system available
    
-   Order withdrawal or recall of system already on market
    
-   Inform Commission and other Member States
    

**Preventive Measures:**

-   Issue warnings and guidance
    
-   Require information and documentation
    
-   Conduct inspections and audits
    
-   Request testing and evaluation
    
-   Mandate corrective action plans
    

## Cross-Border Enforcement

**Mutual Assistance:** Member States shall provide mutual assistance for enforcement activities

**Information Sharing:**

-   Market surveillance authorities share information through EU database
    
-   Alert mechanisms for serious risks
    
-   Coordination through European AI Board
    

**Joint Investigations:**

-   Multiple Member States may coordinate on cross-border cases
    
-   AI Office may facilitate coordination
    
-   Harmonized enforcement approach
    

## Criminal Liability

**Beyond administrative fines:** Member States may establish criminal liability for serious violations under national law

**Areas where criminal liability may apply:**

-   Intentional deployment of prohibited AI systems
    
-   Systematic non-compliance causing serious harm
    
-   Fraudulent certification or documentation
    
-   Obstruction of regulatory investigations

## IMPLEMENTATION TIMELINE

The EU AI Act has a phased implementation schedule with staggered application dates.

## 2024

**July 12, 2024:** AI Act published in Official Journal of the European Union

**August 1, 2024:** AI Act enters into force (Day 1)

-   No requirements apply yet
    
-   Begins countdown to various application dates
    
-   Organizations should begin gap analysis and preparation
    

**November 2, 2024 (3 months):** Member States deadline

-   Identify and publicly list fundamental rights protection authorities
    
-   Notify Commission and other Member States of designated authorities
    

## 2025

**February 2, 2025 (6 months):** First substantive obligations apply

-   **Prohibited AI systems:** Ban on unacceptable risk AI systems takes effect (Article 5)
    
-   **AI literacy requirements:** Obligations for AI literacy apply (Article 4)
    
-   Organizations must immediately cease use of prohibited systems
    
-   Penalties for prohibited systems become enforceable
    

**May 2, 2025 (9 months):** Code of Practice deadline

-   Codes of practice for GPAI models must be ready
    
-   Developed through multi-stakeholder process
    

**August 2, 2025 (12 months):** Major governance and GPAI obligations

-   **GPAI obligations apply:** Providers of general-purpose AI models must comply (Chapter V)
    
    -   Standard GPAI obligations (documentation, copyright, transparency)
        
    -   Systemic risk GPAI obligations (evaluation, risk mitigation, incident reporting, cybersecurity)
        
-   **Governance structures operational:** (Chapter VII)
    
    -   European AI Board first meeting
        
    -   Advisory Forum established
        
    -   Scientific Panel operational
        
    -   National competent authorities must be designated and operational
        
-   **Notified bodies:** Framework for notified bodies applies (Chapter III, Section 4)
    
-   **Confidentiality obligations:** Apply to all entities (Article 78)
    
-   **Penalties framework:** Administrative fines become applicable (Articles 99-100)
    

**August 2, 2025 (annually thereafter):**

-   Commission annual review of prohibited practices
    
-   Possible amendments to prohibition list based on technological developments
    

## 2026

**February 2, 2026 (18 months):** Commission guidelines

-   Commission must provide guidelines on Article 6 implementation
    
-   Guidance on high-risk classification
    
-   Post-market monitoring plan specifications
    

**August 2, 2026 (24 months):** HIGH-RISK AI SYSTEMS OBLIGATIONS APPLY  
**This is the main compliance deadline for most organizations**

-   **High-risk AI systems under Annex III:** Full compliance required for stand-alone high-risk systems
    
    -   Risk management systems
        
    -   Data governance
        
    -   Technical documentation
        
    -   Record-keeping and logging
        
    -   Transparency and user information
        
    -   Human oversight measures
        
    -   Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity
        
    -   Quality management systems
        
    -   Conformity assessment
        
    -   EU database registration
        
    -   CE marking
        
    -   Post-market monitoring
        
-   **Deployers of high-risk AI systems:** Must comply with deployment obligations
    
-   **Existing high-risk AI systems:** Systems placed on market or put into service before this date must comply only if subject to significant design changes after this date
    
-   **Full enforcement powers:** AI Office gains full enforcement authority over GPAI models, including:
    
    -   Information requests
        
    -   Model evaluation orders
        
    -   Corrective measures
        
    -   Administrative fines
        

## 2027

**August 2, 2027 (36 months):** High-risk AI safety components

-   **High-risk AI systems under Annex I:** Compliance required for AI as safety component of regulated products
    
-   **Legacy GPAI models:** Providers of GPAI models placed on market before August 2, 2025, must have achieved full compliance
    
-   **Large-scale IT systems:** AI systems that are components of large-scale IT systems listed in Annex X (placed on market or put into service before this date) must be brought into compliance (but have until December 31, 2030)
    

## 2030

**August 2, 2030 (6 years):** Public authority high-risk AI

-   **Public authorities using high-risk AI:** Providers and deployers of high-risk AI systems intended for use by public authorities must have achieved full compliance
    
-   Recognizes longer procurement and implementation cycles in public sector
    

**December 31, 2030:** Large-scale IT systems final deadline

-   AI systems that are components of large-scale IT systems listed in Annex X (placed on market or put into service before August 2, 2027) must achieve full compliance
    

## 2031

**August 2, 2031 (7 years):** Comprehensive evaluation

-   Commission shall carry out assessment of Act enforcement
    
-   Report to European Parliament, Council, and European Economic and Social Committee
    
-   May propose amendments based on technological developments and practical experience
    

## Key Takeaways on Timeline

**Immediate actions (2024-2025):**

-   Stop using prohibited AI systems
    
-   Classify existing AI systems by risk level
    
-   Begin gap analysis against requirements
    
-   Prepare for GPAI obligations if applicable
    

**Priority deadline - August 2, 2026:**

-   Most high-risk AI systems must be fully compliant
    
-   24 months from entry into force
    
-   Main compliance deadline for majority of organizations
    

**Extended timelines:**

-   Safety component high-risk AI: August 2027 (36 months)
    
-   Legacy GPAI models: August 2027
    
-   Public authority systems: August 2030
    

**Continuous obligations:**

-   Annual review of prohibitions
    
-   Ongoing monitoring and reporting requirements
    
-   Regular updates to documentation and systems

## CONFORMITY ASSESSMENT PROCEDURES

For high-risk AI systems, demonstrating compliance requires following structured conformity assessment procedures.

## Internal Control Assessment (Annex VI)

**Applicable to:** Most high-risk AI systems under Annex III (not all)

**Process:**

1.  **Technical documentation preparation:** Provider prepares comprehensive technical documentation per Annex IV
    
2.  **Self-assessment:** Provider assesses system's compliance with Chapter III, Section 2 requirements
    
3.  **Quality management system:** Implement and maintain quality management system per Article 17
    
4.  **EU declaration of conformity:** Draw up declaration per Annex V
    
5.  **CE marking:** Affix CE marking to the system or its documentation
    
6.  **Registration:** Register system in EU database
    

**Responsibility:** Provider conducts entire assessment internally

**No third-party involvement:** Provider self-certifies compliance

**Documentation requirements:**

-   Must maintain technical documentation for 10 years after AI system placed on market or put into service
    
-   Must be available to market surveillance authorities upon request
    
-   Must be updated when changes made to system
    

## Assessment Based on Quality Management System and Technical Documentation (Annex VII)

**Applicable to:**

-   Certain high-risk AI systems requiring enhanced scrutiny
    
-   High-risk AI systems under Annex I (safety components)
    
-   Specific high-risk categories requiring third-party assessment
    

**Process:**

**Stage 1: Quality Management System Assessment**

1.  Provider implements quality management system per Article 17
    
2.  Provider applies to notified body for assessment
    
3.  Notified body:
    
    -   Reviews quality management system documentation
        
    -   Conducts audit of quality management system
        
    -   Examines provider's processes, procedures, and controls
        
    -   May conduct on-site assessment
        
4.  Notified body issues certificate if quality management system complies
    

**Stage 2: Technical Documentation Assessment**

1.  Provider prepares technical documentation per Annex IV
    
2.  Provider submits technical documentation to notified body
    
3.  Notified body:
    
    -   Reviews technical documentation for completeness and accuracy
        
    -   Assesses conformity with requirements
        
    -   May request additional information or testing
        
    -   Examines risk management, data governance, and technical measures
        
4.  Notified body issues EU technical documentation assessment certificate if documentation demonstrates compliance
    

**Stage 3: Post-Assessment**

1.  Provider draws up EU declaration of conformity
    
2.  Provider affixes CE marking
    
3.  Provider registers system in EU database
    

**Surveillance:** Notified body conducts periodic surveillance assessments (typically annual)

## CE Marking Requirements

**Application:** Required for all high-risk AI systems that have undergone successful conformity assessment

**Format:**

-   Must follow design requirements in Regulation (EC) No 765/2008
    
-   Must be visible, legible, and indelible
    
-   Minimum height: 5mm (unless otherwise specified for product category)
    

**Placement:**

-   On the AI system (where physically possible)
    
-   On its packaging
    
-   In accompanying documentation
    
-   On digital interface if system is software-only
    

**Accompanied by:**

-   Identification number of notified body (if third-party assessment conducted)
    
-   Name and address of provider
    
-   Contact information
    

**Meaning:**

-   Declares conformity with all applicable EU requirements
    
-   Indicates system may be made available on EU market
    
-   Provider takes responsibility for compliance
    

**Prohibitions:**

-   Affixing CE marking to non-compliant systems (subject to penalties)
    
-   Misleading marks or logos that could be confused with CE marking
    

## EU Declaration of Conformity

**Required for:** All high-risk AI systems

**Content must include:**

-   Provider's name and address
    
-   AI system name, type, version
    
-   Statement that AI system complies with the Act
    
-   References to applicable harmonized standards or technical specifications
    
-   Name and identification number of notified body (if applicable)
    
-   Place and date of issue
    
-   Signature of authorized person
    

**Languages:** Must be provided in language(s) required by Member State(s) where system is made available

**Availability:**

-   Must be kept available for market surveillance authorities
    
-   Typically for 10 years after system placed on market
    
-   May be required to accompany the system

## REGULATORY SANDBOXES

The EU AI Act establishes AI regulatory sandboxes to foster innovation while ensuring compliance.

## Definition and Purpose (Article 57)

**Regulatory sandbox:** A controlled environment established by a competent authority that facilitates the development, testing, and validation of innovative AI systems for a limited time before their placement on the market or putting into service.

**Objectives:**

-   Foster AI innovation, especially by SMEs and start-ups
    
-   Enable testing in real-world conditions under regulatory supervision
    
-   Enhance legal certainty for innovators
    
-   Support evidence-based regulatory learning
    
-   Facilitate development of harmonized standards
    
-   Promote cooperation between innovators and competent authorities
    

## Establishment and Operation

**Establishment:** Member States shall ensure regulatory sandboxes are established by August 2, 2025

**Authority:** May be established at national or regional level by competent authorities

**Access criteria:**

-   Open to providers and prospective providers
    
-   Selection based on:
    
    -   Innovation potential
        
    -   Relevance to regulatory objectives
        
    -   Feasibility of testing plan
        
    -   Resource requirements
        
-   Priority consideration for SMEs and start-ups
    

## Participation Benefits

**For participants:**

-   **Reduced entry barriers:** Lower compliance costs during development phase
    
-   **Regulatory guidance:** Direct access to supervisory authority expertise
    
-   **Testing flexibility:** Ability to test under real-world conditions with appropriate safeguards
    
-   **Legal certainty:** Clear understanding of requirements and compliance pathway
    
-   **Documentation support:** Assistance in preparing technical documentation
    
-   **Fast-track pathway:** Streamlined route to market after successful testing
    

**Safeguards required:**

-   Appropriate risk mitigation measures
    
-   Informed consent from users or subjects
    
-   Clear exit strategy if risks materialize
    
-   Supervision by competent authority
    
-   Time-limited participation (typically 12-24 months)
    

## Sandbox Scope

**Permissible activities:**

-   Development and training of AI systems
    
-   Testing and validation under supervision
    
-   Real-world deployment with appropriate safeguards
    
-   Data collection for training and testing
    
-   User interaction studies
    
-   Performance evaluation
    

**Not permitted:**

-   Testing of prohibited AI systems
    
-   Activities posing unacceptable risks to fundamental rights
    
-   Operations without adequate safeguards

## HARMONIZED STANDARDS AND TECHNICAL SPECIFICATIONS

## Role of Harmonized Standards

**Purpose:** Provide presumption of conformity with Act requirements

**Development:** Created by European standardization organizations (CEN, CENELEC, ETSI) based on standardization requests from the Commission

**Voluntary but incentivized:**

-   Following harmonized standards creates presumption of conformity
    
-   Simplified compliance demonstration
    
-   Reduced conformity assessment burden
    

**Key areas for standardization:**

-   Risk management methodologies
    
-   Data quality and governance
    
-   Technical documentation structure
    
-   Transparency and explainability
    
-   Human oversight mechanisms
    
-   Robustness and accuracy testing
    
-   Cybersecurity measures
    
-   Quality management systems
    

## Common Specifications

**When used:** If no harmonized standards exist or existing standards insufficient

**Adoption:** Commission may adopt common specifications via implementing acts

**Mandatory nature:** Compliance with common specifications may be mandatory in absence of harmonized standards

**Development process:**

-   Consultation with stakeholders
    
-   Technical expert input
    
-   Public comment period
    
-   Formal adoption by Commission

## INTERNATIONAL DIMENSIONS

## Extraterritorial Application

The EU AI Act applies to:

**Providers and deployers located in the EU:**

-   All AI systems placed on EU market
    
-   All AI systems put into service in EU
    

**Providers and deployers located outside the EU:**

-   When AI system's output is used in the EU
    
-   When deployers are located in the EU
    
-   Regardless of provider location if market impact in EU
    

**Implications:**

-   Global AI companies must comply even if headquartered outside EU
    
-   Cloud-based AI services accessible in EU are covered
    
-   API-based AI services used by EU entities are covered
    
-   Compliance required for international companies serving EU customers
    

## Authorized Representatives for Non-EU Providers

**Requirement:** Providers established outside EU must appoint authorized representative located within EU

**Authorized representative responsibilities:**

-   Act on behalf of provider for compliance purposes
    
-   Maintain technical documentation
    
-   Respond to authority requests
    
-   Cooperate with market surveillance authorities
    
-   Serve as point of contact for EU authorities
    

**Selection:** Must have written mandate from provider

## International Cooperation

**Commission empowered to:**

-   Conclude international agreements on AI governance
    
-   Facilitate mutual recognition of conformity assessments
    
-   Coordinate with international organizations (OECD, Council of Europe, UN)
    
-   Promote international AI standards
    
-   Share best practices and regulatory approaches
    

**Objective:** Position EU AI Act as global standard, similar to GDPR's international influence

## RELATIONSHIP WITH OTHER EU LEGISLATION

## Data Protection (GDPR)

**Complementary relationship:** AI Act and GDPR work together when AI systems process personal data

**Key intersections:**

-   **Data governance:** AI Act data quality requirements complement GDPR data minimization and accuracy principles
    
-   **Rights of data subjects:** GDPR rights (access, erasure, rectification) apply to personal data in AI systems
    
-   **Transparency:** Both require transparency about automated decision-making
    
-   **Human oversight:** GDPR Article 22 (right not to be subject to solely automated decision-making) complements AI Act human oversight requirements
    
-   **Impact assessments:** AI Act fundamental rights impact assessments may overlap with GDPR data protection impact assessments
    
-   **Supervisory authorities:** Data protection authorities coordinate with AI Act market surveillance authorities
    

**No conflict:** Where both apply, must comply with both frameworks

## Product Safety Legislation

**Harmonized approach:** AI Act integrates with existing product safety framework

**High-risk AI as safety components:**

-   AI systems that are safety components of products covered by EU product safety legislation (Annex I) are high-risk
    
-   Must comply with both AI Act requirements and sector-specific product regulations
    

**Examples of covered products:**

-   Medical devices (MDR, IVDR)
    
-   Machinery (Machinery Directive)
    
-   Toys (Toy Safety Directive)
    
-   Automotive (Type Approval Regulation)
    
-   Aviation (Aviation Safety Regulation)
    

**Coordinated conformity assessment:** Single integrated assessment covers both AI Act and product-specific requirements

## Cybersecurity Legislation

**NIS2 Directive:** Cybersecurity requirements for AI systems align with Network and Information Security Directive

**Cyber Resilience Act:** Coordination on cybersecurity requirements for digital products including AI systems

**Complementary obligations:** Both frameworks require appropriate cybersecurity measures

## Sector-Specific Legislation

**Financial services:**

-   AI Act complements existing financial services regulations
    
-   High-risk classification for creditworthiness assessment aligns with consumer credit directives
    

**Healthcare:**

-   Medical device regulations remain applicable
    
-   AI Act provides additional layer for AI-specific risks
    

**Employment:**

-   AI Act reinforces EU employment law protections
    
-   High-risk classification for HR AI systems ensures fundamental rights protection
    

**Law enforcement:**

-   Law Enforcement Directive remains applicable
    
-   AI Act provides specific requirements for law enforcement AI use

## PRACTICAL COMPLIANCE GUIDANCE

## Step-by-Step Compliance Roadmap

## Phase 1: Initial Assessment (Months 1-2)

**1. Inventory AI systems:**

-   Identify all AI systems developed, deployed, or used
    
-   Document intended purpose of each system
    
-   Classify whether system or GPAI model
    

**2. Risk classification:**

-   Determine if system is prohibited (immediate cessation required)
    
-   Assess if system is high-risk (check Annexes I and III)
    
-   Evaluate if GPAI model and whether systemic risk
    
-   Identify if limited or minimal risk
    

**3. Role identification:**

-   Determine if acting as provider, deployer, distributor, importer, or multiple roles
    
-   Identify authorized representative needs (if provider outside EU)
    

**4. Gap analysis:**

-   Compare current practices against applicable requirements
    
-   Identify compliance gaps
    
-   Assess documentation readiness
    
-   Evaluate technical and organizational measures
    

## Phase 2: Planning and Preparation (Months 3-6)

**5. Develop compliance strategy:**

-   Prioritize AI systems by risk level and timeline
    
-   Allocate resources (budget, personnel, technology)
    
-   Assign responsibilities and accountability
    
-   Establish governance structure
    

**6. Technical preparations:**

-   Implement or enhance risk management systems
    
-   Improve data governance practices
    
-   Develop logging and record-keeping capabilities
    
-   Strengthen cybersecurity measures
    
-   Design human oversight mechanisms
    

**7. Documentation development:**

-   Create technical documentation templates
    
-   Prepare quality management system documentation
    
-   Develop user instructions
    
-   Draft EU declarations of conformity (templates)
    

**8. Process establishment:**

-   Set up conformity assessment procedures
    
-   Establish post-market monitoring processes
    
-   Create incident reporting workflows
    
-   Develop change management procedures
    

## Phase 3: Implementation (Months 7-18)

**9. Execute technical measures:**

-   Implement risk management systems
    
-   Enhance data quality and governance
    
-   Deploy logging infrastructure
    
-   Integrate human oversight mechanisms
    
-   Strengthen accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity
    

**10. Create documentation:**

-   Complete technical documentation for each high-risk system
    
-   Finalize quality management system documentation
    
-   Prepare user instructions and transparency information
    

**11. Conformity assessment:**

-   Conduct internal assessments (if self-assessment permitted)
    
-   Engage notified bodies (if third-party assessment required)
    
-   Address any findings or deficiencies
    
-   Obtain certificates as applicable
    

**12. Registration and marking:**

-   Register high-risk AI systems in EU database
    
-   Affix CE marking to compliant systems
    
-   Issue EU declarations of conformity
    

## Phase 4: Ongoing Compliance (Continuous)

**13. Post-market monitoring:**

-   Implement continuous monitoring of AI system performance
    
-   Track incidents and near-misses
    
-   Collect user feedback
    
-   Monitor for emerging risks
    

**14. Incident management:**

-   Respond promptly to incidents and malfunctions
    
-   Report serious incidents to authorities as required
    
-   Implement corrective actions
    
-   Document incident response activities
    

**15. Maintenance and updates:**

-   Keep technical documentation current
    
-   Update systems to address identified issues
    
-   Maintain quality management system
    
-   Conduct periodic internal audits
    

**16. Regulatory engagement:**

-   Respond to authority information requests
    
-   Cooperate with market surveillance investigations
    
-   Participate in regulatory sandboxes if applicable
    
-   Monitor regulatory developments and guidance

## CONCLUSION

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act represents a comprehensive, risk-based approach to AI governance that balances innovation with protection of fundamental rights, safety, and security. As the world's first comprehensive AI legislation, it establishes important precedents and frameworks that are likely to influence AI regulation globally.

**Key takeaways:**

1.  **Risk-based approach:** Proportional regulation based on potential harms
    
2.  **Broad applicability:** Covers providers and deployers globally when AI affects EU
    
3.  **Severe penalties:** Up to €35 million or 7% of global revenue for serious violations
    
4.  **Phased implementation:** Staggered deadlines from 2025-2030, with main deadline August 2026
    
5.  **GPAI focus:** Specific framework for foundation models with enhanced obligations for systemic risk
    
6.  **Multi-level governance:** European and national authorities with clear roles
    
7.  **Innovation support:** Regulatory sandboxes and SME provisions
    
8.  **Living regulation:** Annual reviews and amendment mechanisms
    

Organizations developing, deploying, or using AI systems must proactively assess their AI portfolios, classify systems appropriately, implement required technical and organizational measures, and establish robust compliance programs to meet the Act's requirements within applicable timelines.
